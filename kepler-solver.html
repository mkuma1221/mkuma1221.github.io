Kepler’s Problem Solver

Project Overview and Goals

  • Validate and compare two orbital propagation methods: analytical Keplerian propagation and numerical integration under ideal two-body orbital dynamics.
  • Compute the spacecraft’s Classical Orbital Elements (COEs) from an initial Earth-Centered Inertial (ECI) state vector.
  • Apply the Newton–Raphson method to solve Kepler’s Equation and generate a high-accuracy analytical reference solution over five orbital periods.
  • Implement MATLAB’s adaptive ode45 solver to numerically integrate the two-body equations of motion using error tolerances of 10-2, 10-6, and 10-10.
  • Analyze how numerical solver tolerance affects orbital propagation accuracy and long-term solution stability.
  • Compare ECI position and velocity histories between analytical and numerical solutions.
  • Visualize the spacecraft trajectory in three-dimensional space.
  • Evaluate conservation of specific mechanical energy and specific angular momentum to quantify numerical drift and integration accuracy.

Methodology

  • The project began with an initial Earth-Centered Inertial (ECI) position and velocity state vector at t = 0.
  • Classical Orbital Elements (COEs) were computed from the initial state vectors.
  • The orbit was characterized using specific orbital energy, angular momentum, and eccentricity vectors.
  • A Direction Cosine Matrix (DCM) was used to transform between ECI and perifocal frames.
  • Keplerian propagation was performed using mean motion and orbital period relationships.
  • Kepler’s Equation was solved using Newton–Raphson iteration with the mean anomaly as the initial guess.
  • Perifocal state vectors were transformed back into ECI using the DCM.
  • ODE45 was used for numerical propagation with multiple tolerance levels.

Results and Discussion

  • The results compare analytical Kepler propagation with numerical ODE45 integration under different solver tolerances.

Comparison of ECI State Vectors

The state vector comparison shows strong agreement between analytical and numerical solutions. The ODE45 solution with tighter tolerances closely follows the Keplerian reference. At higher tolerances, small deviations accumulate over time, particularly in velocity components, due to numerical integration error in solving the two-body equations of motion.

Specific Orbital Energy vs Time

The specific orbital energy remains constant in the analytical solution, as expected for a conservative two-body system. The numerical solution shows slight drift, especially for the $10^{-2}$ tolerance case. As tolerance is tightened, energy conservation improves significantly, confirming higher numerical accuracy.

Specific Angular Momentum vs Time

The magnitude of specific angular momentum remains nearly constant for the analytical solution. Numerical integration introduces small variations, primarily in the loose tolerance case. For tighter tolerances, angular momentum is well preserved, confirming correct implementation of the two-body dynamics.

Overall, the results demonstrate that the Newton–Raphson Kepler solution provides a highly accurate reference, while ODE45 performance strongly depends on tolerance selection. Both energy and angular momentum conservation confirm the physical correctness of the propagation methods.
